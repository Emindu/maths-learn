binomial_distribution

discrete  ·  support: {0, 1, ..., n}  ·  parameters: n ∈ ℕ, p ∈ [0, 1]

USE CASES
⚖️
Bayesian Inference
Model k successes in n trials with unknown p. With a Beta(α,β) prior on p, the posterior is Beta(α+k, β+n−k) — the Beta-Binomial conjugate pair used throughout Bayesian statistics.
🧪
A/B Testing
Count conversions out of n page visits. Compare Binomial counts across control and treatment to determine statistical significance of conversion rate differences.
⚾
Batting Averages
Model hits in n at-bats with hit probability p. Inference on batting average uncertainty naturally uses the Binomial likelihood paired with a Beta prior on p.
🗳️
Election Forecasting
Predict vote counts among n voters given polling proportion p. Aggregating Binomial samples across demographic strata underpins modern probabilistic election models.

RELATIONSHIPS
→
Bernoulli(p)
Special case n = 1. The Binomial is the sum of n independent Bernoulli(p) trials — Binomial(1, p) ≡ Bernoulli(p).
→
Poisson(λ)
Limiting approximation: Binomial(n, p) → Poisson(np) as n → ∞ and p → 0 with np = λ fixed. Most accurate for rare events (p < 0.05, n > 20).
→
Normal(np, np(1−p))
CLT approximation: Binomial(n, p) ≈ Normal(np, np(1−p)) when np > 5 and n(1−p) > 5. Continuity correction improves accuracy.
→
Negative Binomial
Counts trials until r successes. Generalises the Geometric distribution (r=1). Related to Binomial via the count/waiting-time duality.
→
Beta(α, β) — Conjugate Prior
The Beta is the conjugate prior for the Binomial success probability p. Observing k successes in n trials updates Beta(α,β) → Beta(α+k, β+n−k).

PMF & CDF FORMULAS

Probability Mass Function

\[ P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}, \quad k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, n\} \]

Cumulative Distribution Function (via regularised incomplete Beta)

\[ F(k;\,n,p) = I_{1-p}(n-k,\; k+1) = \sum_{i=0}^{k} \binom{n}{i} p^i (1-p)^{n-i} \]

Moment Generating Function

\[ M_X(t) = \bigl(1 - p + p\,e^t\bigr)^n \]
PARAMETER EFFECTS

n (number of trials): Increasing n shifts the mean rightward (μ = np) and widens the distribution (σ = √(np(1−p))). The relative spread, however, shrinks — the coefficient of variation 1/√(np) → 0, so more trials concentrate the distribution around its mean.

p (success probability): Controls both location and asymmetry. At p = 0.5 the distribution is perfectly symmetric (skewness = 0). When p < 0.5, the distribution is right-skewed; when p > 0.5, left-skewed. Near p = 0 or p = 1, the mass concentrates at the extremes and the distribution is highly skewed.

MEAN & VARIANCE DERIVATIONS
── MEAN ──
\(\displaystyle E[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}\)
Apply identity \(k\binom{n}{k} = n\binom{n-1}{k-1}\), then re-index the sum:
\(\displaystyle = np \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{j} p^j (1-p)^{n-1-j} = np \cdot 1 = \mathbf{np}\)
── VARIANCE ──
Use \(\text{Var}(X) = E[X(X-1)] + E[X] - (E[X])^2\)
By a similar identity: \(E[X(X-1)] = n(n-1)p^2\)
\(\text{Var}(X) = n(n-1)p^2 + np - n^2p^2 = np(1-p) = \mathbf{np(1-p)}\)
CONJUGATE PRIOR UPDATE RULE
── BETA – BINOMIAL CONJUGACY ──
Prior:     p ~ Beta(α, β)
Likelihood: X | p ~ Binomial(n, p)
Posterior:  p | X=k ~ Beta(α + k, β + n − k)

Each observed success increments α; each failure increments β. The posterior mean (α+k)/(α+β+n) interpolates between the prior mean α/(α+β) and the MLE k/n, shrinking toward the prior when n is small and toward the data when n is large.
