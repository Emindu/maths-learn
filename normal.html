ABOUT

The Normal (Gaussian) distribution is the most important probability distribution in statistics. Its characteristic bell curve shape — symmetric, unimodal, with tails that decay rapidly — arises naturally wherever a quantity is the sum of many small independent effects. This is formalised by the Central Limit Theorem.

The distribution is fully determined by two parameters: μ shifts the centre and σ controls the spread. The 68-95-99.7 rule captures this compactly: about 68% of probability falls within ±1σ of the mean, 95% within ±2σ, and 99.7% within ±3σ. Mean, median, and mode are all equal to μ, reflecting perfect symmetry.

Many important distributions are derived from the Normal. Squaring a standard Normal gives Chi-squared; ratios of Normals give t and F distributions; the logarithm of a lognormal is normal. In Bayesian inference, the Normal is self-conjugate: a Normal prior combined with a Normal likelihood gives a Normal posterior.
