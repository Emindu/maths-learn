Continuous Distribution

Beta Distribution

A flexible distribution on [0, 1] — the natural home of probabilities, proportions, and rates.

Real-World Use Cases

Any time you model an unknown probability or a proportion bounded between 0 and 1, the Beta distribution is the natural choice.

🧪

Bayesian Inference

The Beta distribution is the conjugate prior for Bernoulli and Binomial likelihoods. Updating beliefs after observing successes and failures is just updating α and β.

📊

Conversion Rates

A/B test conversion rates are modelled as Beta distributions. After observing clicks and non-clicks, the Beta posterior captures uncertainty about the true rate.

🏏

Batting Averages

A cricket or baseball batting average is a proportion in [0, 1]. Empirical Bayes methods use the Beta distribution to smooth estimates for players with few at-bats.

🗳️

Election Forecasting

A candidate's vote share is a proportion. Forecasters place a Beta prior over vote share and update it as poll data arrives.

Key Properties at a Glance

  • Defined on the open interval (0, 1) — perfect for modelling probabilities and proportions.
  • Two shape parameters α and β control the exact shape — symmetric, skewed, uniform, or U-shaped.
  • When α = β = 1 the distribution is perfectly uniform — all proportions equally likely.
  • When α > 1 and β > 1 the distribution is bell-shaped (unimodal).
  • When α < 1 and β < 1 the distribution is U-shaped (bimodal, mass at extremes).
  • When α < 1 and β > 1 (or vice versa) the distribution is J-shaped (monotone).
  • The conjugate prior property makes Bayesian updating trivially simple: Beta(α, β) + n successes, m failures = Beta(α+n, β+m).

Probability Density Function (PDF)

A random variable X follows a Beta distribution with parameters α > 0 and β > 0 if its density on (0, 1) is:

\[ f(x;\,\alpha,\beta) = \frac{x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha,\beta)}, \quad 0 < x < 1 \]

where the Beta function B(α, β) is the normalising constant:

\[ B(\alpha,\beta) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\,\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)} = \int_0^1 t^{\alpha-1}(1-t)^{\beta-1}\,dt \]

and Γ is the Gamma function (a generalisation of the factorial: Γ(n) = (n−1)! for positive integers).

Effect of the Shape Parameters

α = β = 1: The Beta(1, 1) distribution is the Uniform[0, 1] — complete ignorance about the value of x.

α = β > 1: The distribution is symmetric and bell-shaped, concentrated around 0.5. Higher values produce a narrower peak.

α > β: The distribution is left-skewed — mass is concentrated nearer 1 (high probability values are more likely).

α < β: The distribution is right-skewed — mass is concentrated nearer 0 (low probability values are more likely).

α < 1 and β < 1: The distribution is U-shaped — most mass near 0 and 1, as in the arcsine distribution (α = β = 0.5).

Concentration α + β: The total concentration controls how "peaked" the distribution is. Larger α + β means less variance — useful to interpret how much information a Beta prior encodes.

Derivation of Mean and Variance

Mean (Expected Value): Using the integral definition and the identity B(α+1, β) = α·B(α, β) / (α+β):

\[ E[X] = \int_0^1 x \cdot \frac{x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha,\beta)}\,dx = \frac{B(\alpha+1,\beta)}{B(\alpha,\beta)} = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta} \]

Variance: Using E[X²] = B(α+2, β) / B(α, β) = α(α+1) / [(α+β)(α+β+1)]:

\[ \text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)} \]

Bayesian update rule: If the prior is Beta(α, β) and we observe n successes and m failures from a Bernoulli/Binomial experiment, the posterior is simply Beta(α+n, β+m). This is the conjugate prior property.
